O'Sensei Richard Kim

1917 - 2001

O’Sensei Richard Kim (1917–2001) was a renowned martial artist, widely respected for his profound contributions to the world of martial arts. A 10th Dan Hanshi, he was a bridge between the ancient traditions of the East and the modern West, dedicating his life to preserving the "Butoku"—the martial virtues.

"As an artist uses different medium such as clay, paint or music, as a means of artistic impression a martial artist uses their own life as their mode of expression."

O'Sensei Richard Kim was such an artist. His completed artistic work, his life, went far beyond what most people could ever hope to imagine let alone accomplish. He was a judo-ka, a boxer, a soldier, a karate master, a priest, a Ph.D. in oriental philosophy, a writer, and a martial arts sensei.

Early Life & The Boxing Ring

Born in Hawaii on November 17, 1919 (often cited as 1917), Richard Kim began his journey at age six with Judo. By 1927, he was witnessing demonstrations by Okinawan masters like Yabu Kentsu, sparking a lifelong passion for Shorinji Ryu.


As a teenager known as "Biggie" Kim, he dominated the local boxing clubs. He learned about focus and the jab from world contenders, eventually fighting 42 professional bouts in the Orient with a record of 42-0. He became the "Champion of the Orient," yet learned the limitations of sport fighting when he saw a boxer defeated by a wrestler.

The Warrior's Education

In 1939, Kim worked on a ship to travel to Japan. Upon arrival, he trained under the rigorous tutelage of Yoshida Kotaro in Daito Ryu. Yoshida, renowned for his weapons skills, taught Kim spear, sword, and the internal arts. Kim eventually received the Menkyo Kaiden—a certificate of full proficiency—from Yoshida in 1952.


His education continued in China, where he learned Tai Chi and Pa Kua, and studied at St. John's University. He was a man of both the pen and the sword, later owning the "Light House" bar in Yokohama where legends like Mas Oyama would visit to train.

The Classical Man in the West

In 1959, O'Sensei moved to San Francisco and began teaching at the Chinese YMCA. He limited his classes to 50 students, maintaining a high standard of discipline. He founded the Zen Bei Butoku-Kai to preserve the traditions he had mastered.


He was a prolific writer, authoring the column "The Classical Man" in Karate Illustrated. He used his life as a lesson, teaching history, philosophy, and strategy. As he famously said:

"If you sleep more than four hours a day, you lose."

His work ethic was legendary, and his personal development was a result of this tireless dedication.

Masters & Lineage

O'Sensei Kim's knowledge was a convergence of the greatest martial minds of the 20th century. He trained directly under veritable legends:

Yoshida Kotaro

Daito Ryu Aiki-jujutsu

His principal teacher who awarded him Menkyo Kaiden. A master of the internal arts and weaponry.

Yabu Kentsu

Shorinji Ryu Karate

The "Sergeant" of Okinawan Karate, student of Matsumura and Itosu. Known for his intense discipline.

Wang Xian Zhai

Yi Quan

The founder of Yi Quan (Mind Boxing). Taught O'Sensei the internal power of standing meditation.

Gogen Yamaguchi

Goju Ryu

The "Cat" of Japanese Karate. O'Sensei trained with him in Yokohama.

The Legacy Continues

O’Sensei Richard Kim passed away on November 8, 2001. But his work did not end there. Before his passing, he ensured the torch was passed to his senior student, Sensei Brian Ricci.


Today, the Zen Bei Butoku-Kai International continues to thrive. The annual summer camp at Guelph University, a tradition O'Sensei loved, continues under Sensei Ricci's leadership. The organization remains a guardian of the traditional martial arts, ensuring O'Sensei's knowledge and wisdom continue into the next generation. As the saying goes, his life serves as a legend for us all.

Wisdom & Philosophy

O'Sensei taught that "Patience is the ballast of the soul." He emphasized Mushin ("no mind")—seeing things as they truly are, not as we wish them to be. He would often ask students, "Do you see what you see?" reminding them to look beyond the surface.


One of his favorite maxims was "Ichi go, ichi e" (One moment, one lifetime). "When you clap your hands, you can never get that moment back," he taught. "Take care of now, and the future will take care of itself."


The Path of Character

"Watch your thoughts, they become words.
Watch your words, they become actions.
Watch your actions, they become habits.
Watch your habits, they become character.
Watch your character, it becomes your destiny."

A Guiding Light

"To myself and many of his students he certainly was our 'Guiding Light.' O'Sensei was a great martial arts instructor, but he was also an untiring student. He was constantly studying, practicing and learning throughout his entire life... For you see, O'Sensei was not just training martial artists, he was developing artists of life."

"O'Sensei's dedication to learning and then passing on that knowledge serves as a wonderful inspiration... He has left us with the lesson to always keep the 'Beginner's Mind,' to never stop learning."

- Sensei Rod Sanford
